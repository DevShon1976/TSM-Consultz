What WIP means

WIP means Work In Progress. In BPO services, WIP figures show how much operational work is currently open, aging, delayed, blocked, risky, or ready for action.

What WIP tells the client

  • How much work is sitting in the queue.
  • Which work is aging or overdue.
  • Where manual review is slowing the team down.
  • Where revenue, compliance, or project risk is building.
  • Who needs to own the next action.

How to explain it simply

WIP is the live picture of unfinished work.
The value is not just knowing the count.
The value is knowing which items need action first.

When to mention WIP

Mention WIP early when showing the dashboard, then come back to it after BNCA to prove that the system turns unfinished work into prioritized next actions.

Chart A — Queue Volume

Chart A should show how many items are currently in the workflow queue.

What it means

  • Total open work.
  • Backlog pressure.
  • Department load.
  • Whether volume is rising or falling.

Say this

Chart A shows the current workload.
This tells leadership whether the team is managing a normal queue or an overloaded queue.

When to highlight it

  • At the beginning of the demo.
  • When explaining why the client needs visibility.
  • When discussing staffing, backlog, or throughput.

Chart B — SLA Aging

Chart B should show how long work items have been open and which ones are at risk of breaching SLA.

What it means

  • Which items are becoming overdue.
  • Where client service risk is rising.
  • Which queue needs escalation.
  • Which owner lane needs attention.

Say this

Chart B is the urgency chart.
It tells us which work is not just open, but aging into risk.

When to highlight it

  • When selling SLA governance.
  • When discussing missed deadlines.
  • When showing why BNCA matters.

Chart C — Risk / Exception Count

Chart C should show how many work items contain issues, exceptions, missing evidence, or risk flags.

What it means

  • Missing documents.
  • Compliance exposure.
  • Financial variance.
  • Delayed approvals.
  • Client-impacting blockers.

Say this

Chart C shows where normal work turns into risk.
This is where TSM helps prioritize what needs review first.

When to highlight it

  • Right before running BNCA.
  • When explaining why manual review misses things.
  • When transitioning into AI Analyst output.

Chart D — Owner Lane / Assignment

Chart D shows who owns the work and whether anything is unassigned.

Say this

Chart D answers the accountability question:
Who owns this next?

Chart E — Financial / Recovery Impact

Chart E shows dollars connected to open work: invoice exposure, recovery opportunity, AR risk, change order exposure, denial risk, or charge-off prevention.

Say this

Chart E connects operations to money.
This is where the executive sponsor starts seeing ROI.

Chart F — Executive Readiness / Resolution Status

Chart F shows how much of the queue is ready, blocked, escalated, resolved, or pending leadership review.

Say this

Chart F tells leadership whether the workflow is under control or still needs intervention.

Best presentation order

Do not explain every chart at once. Use each chart only when it supports the story you are telling.

Opening dashboard

  • Highlight Chart A: Queue Volume.
  • Highlight Chart B: SLA Aging.
  • Say: “This shows the amount of unfinished work and which items are aging into risk.”

Before AI Analyst

  • Highlight Chart C: Risk / Exceptions.
  • Say: “This is where the system decides what needs review first.”

After BNCA

  • Highlight Chart D: Owner Lane.
  • Highlight Chart F: Resolution Status.
  • Say: “Now the issue has an owner, next action, and escalation path.”

Executive ROI close

  • Highlight Chart E: Financial / Recovery Impact.
  • Say: “This is where operational visibility connects to dollars.”

Your cheat sheet

Chart A = How much work?
Chart B = How old / overdue?
Chart C = What is risky?
Chart D = Who owns it?
Chart E = What is the money impact?
Chart F = Is it resolved, blocked, or escalated?
