Music Business Basics

Most independent artists lose money not because their music isn't good enough, but because they never registered it correctly. These are the basics every artist needs before releasing.

This is general education, not legal or financial advice. For contracts, splits, or anything with real money on the line, talk to a music attorney or a service like DistroKid/CD Baby support.
// CORE CONCEPTS

Copyright

You own the copyright to your song the moment you create it — registration isn't required to own it, but it strengthens your legal position if someone steals it.

U.S. artists can register at copyright.gov for extra protection

Master vs. Publishing

The "master" is the actual recording. "Publishing" is the underlying song — the melody and lyrics. They're owned and paid separately, even for the same track.

As an unsigned artist, you typically own both — until you sign a deal that says otherwise

PRO (Performing Rights Org)

A PRO (like ASCAP or BMI in the U.S.) collects performance royalties when your song is played on radio, TV, or in public. You must register as a songwriter to get paid.

Free to join — sign up before your song drops, not after

Distributor

A distributor (DistroKid, CD Baby, TuneCore) gets your song onto Spotify, Apple Music, etc. They are not a label — they don't promote you, just deliver your files and pay you.

Most charge a flat annual fee and let you keep 100% of royalties

Splits

If multiple people contribute to a song (writer, producer, featured artist), agree on percentage splits in writing BEFORE release — not after it starts making money.

A simple split sheet with signatures prevents almost every future dispute

Sync Licensing

"Sync" means your music placed in a TV show, film, ad, or game. It often pays more per use than streaming and is a real, achievable income source for independent artists.

Keep instrumental/clean versions ready — sync supervisors need them
