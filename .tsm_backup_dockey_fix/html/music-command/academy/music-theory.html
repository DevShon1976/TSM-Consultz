Music Theory Basics

You don't need a music degree to make great records — but knowing these few words will make every conversation with a producer, engineer, or AI coach 10x more useful.

// THE CORE CONCEPTS

BPM (Tempo)

Beats Per Minute — how fast or slow a song feels. Lower BPM feels heavier and more emotional; higher BPM feels energetic and urgent.

Drill: 130–150 BPM  |  Boom bap: 85–95 BPM  |  Pop: 100–125 BPM

Key

The set of notes a song is built around. Minor keys generally sound darker/sadder; major keys generally sound brighter/happier. Your vocal melody and the beat need to share a key or they'll clash.

"This beat is in F Minor" = your melody notes should come from F Minor's scale

Bars

A bar (or "measure") is a chunk of time — usually 4 beats. Verses are commonly 16 bars, hooks are commonly 8 bars. This is how producers and writers communicate song structure quickly.

"Give me 16 on the verse, 8 on the hook" = a standard structure request

Time Signature

How beats are grouped within a bar. The vast majority of hip-hop, pop, R&B, and rock is 4/4 — 4 beats per bar. You will almost never need anything else as a beginner.

4/4 = "four-four time" = the default for nearly everything you'll make

Cadence

The rhythm and flow of how you deliver lyrics over the beat — where you pause, where you rush, where you land on the beat. Two artists can rap the exact same words and sound completely different based on cadence alone.

Try the Cadence Studio to practice this hands-on

Hook vs. Chorus

In hip-hop/R&B, "hook" and "chorus" are often used interchangeably — the most repeated, most memorable part of the song. It should be simple enough to sing back after one listen.

If you can't hum it after hearing it once, it's probably not a hook yet
